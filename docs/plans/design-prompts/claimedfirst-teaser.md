# ClaimedFirst — teaser landing page (Claude Design brief)

## What you are building

A single-page teaser landing page for **ClaimedFirst**, a mobile app about
discovering AI music creators before anyone else does. The app is a concept —
fully designed, not built. This page says almost nothing about how it works.
The mystery is the point. The only goal: make the visitor feel they are about
to be let in on something, and leave an email.

## Background for you, the designer (none of this appears on the page)

- ClaimedFirst is a music discovery game concept. Players claim AI music
  creators; the first to claim an artist becomes Claimer #1, and that claim
  number is permanent and timestamped.
- The emotional hook is "I found them first" — bragging rights for early taste.
- The game rewards genuine taste, early faith, and loyalty. Not spending.
- Use this only to inform mood, word choice, and how the form feels. Do not
  put mechanics, claim numbers, tiers, or explanations anywhere on the page.

## The page, top to bottom (this is the entire page)

1. **Name mark.** ClaimedFirst appears once — a small logo mark at the top or
   worked into the design. Understated, not a banner.
2. **Headline.** One line only. No subheadline. Something in the spirit of
   *"Some people hear them first."* Do **not** use that exact line — write
   something better that carries the same feeling of exclusivity, discovery,
   and bragging rights.
3. **Supporting line.** No more than 8 words. Hints at AI music creators
   without explaining anything. It should make the visitor feel like they are
   about to be let in on something.
4. **Email capture.** A single email field and a submit button. The button
   label should feel like a reward, not a chore. Not "Submit." Not "Sign Up."
   Something like *"Claim My Spot"* — but write one that fits the design and
   feels earned.
5. **Micro-copy.** One short line under the form that reinforces scarcity and
   early access. No spam promises, no legal hedging. Just attitude.

Nothing else. No navigation. No footer links. Nothing to click except the
submit button.

## Design direction

- Dark. Atmospheric. The feeling of a dimly lit venue before a show starts —
  anticipation, not hype.
- Not a tech-startup aesthetic. Not purple gradients. Think editorial. Think
  vinyl. Think the moment before something breaks.
- Background: a sound-adjacent visual texture — a waveform, concentric rings,
  or something abstract that evokes sound without illustrating it. Subtle,
  not literal.
- Typography should feel like it has taste. A display font with character for
  the headline. Something refined and readable for the form. No generic
  system fonts.
- One accent color only. Something unexpected — not white, not gold, not neon
  green. Make a choice that feels like the app has a point of view.
- The email form should feel like signing a guest list, not filling out a
  form. Design it accordingly.
- Mobile-first layout. Centered.
- The page should feel like it was designed by someone who actually loves
  music — not by a startup trying to look like they do.

## Form behavior

- One field, one button. Inline validation only if the email is malformed —
  quiet, no red shouting.
- After submit, the form gives way to a single confirmation line in the same
  voice: short, earned, a little smug. You are on the list; that is all it
  says. No modal, no redirect, no second ask.

## Copy voice

- Plain English. Short sentences. Confidence without hype.
- Every word earns its place. The whole page is roughly 25 words of copy.
- Banned words: leverage, seamless, game-changer, unlock, empower, robust,
  synergy, deep dive. ("Unlock" will tempt you on a page about early access.
  Resist.)

## Do

- Write real, final copy for all five slots above — headline, supporting
  line, button label, micro-copy, confirmation line.
- Treat the email capture as a guest list for something that has not been
  released. Interest and early access, nothing more.
- Spell the name exactly: ClaimedFirst. One word, two capitals.
- Make the submit interaction feel like a small win.
- Keep contrast readable on the dark ground; atmosphere never beats legibility.

## Don't

- No lorem ipsum or placeholder text anywhere.
- No fake user counts, testimonials, press logos, star ratings, or "join
  12,000 others." The product has no users. Do not invent any.
- Nothing that implies the app exists: no app-store badges, no phone mockups
  with screenshots, no "download now," no launch date, no feature list.
- No game mechanics on the page: no claim numbers, rosters, tiers, scores,
  or leaderboards.
- No spam promises ("we'll never share your email") and no legal fine print.
- No navigation, footer, social icons, or secondary links.
- No purple gradients. Accent is not white, not gold, not neon green.
- No song lyrics.

## Done looks like

A visitor on a phone sees one dark, confident screen: a name, a headline that
makes them feel slightly late, eight words that hint, a field, and a button
that feels like staking a claim. They either leave an email or they don't.
Either way they walk away with the feeling: something is coming, and the
people on this list were here first.
